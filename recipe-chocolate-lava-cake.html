Molten Dark Chocolate Lava Cakes

A crackling bittersweet shell gives way to a river of warm molten chocolate the moment you break through. Restaurant-level dessert magic, made in your home oven in under 20 minutes.

About This Recipe

Molten chocolate cake — also known as fondant au chocolat — is the dessert that made Jean-Georges Vongerichten famous in the 1990s. The trick is deceptively simple: underbake a chocolate cake. The exterior sets, the interior stays liquid. The window between "raw batter" and "perfectly molten" is about 90 seconds, which is why understanding your oven is everything here.

The good news: this batter can be made up to two days in advance and refrigerated in the ramekins. When your dinner guests are finishing their main course, you slip the ramekins into the oven. Twelve minutes later, you're a dessert hero. Use the best dark chocolate you can afford — 70% cacao minimum. This is a recipe where ingredient quality is visible in the result.

Ingredients

  • 170g (6 oz) dark chocolate, 70% cacao minimum, finely chopped
  • 115g (½ cup / 1 stick) unsalted butter, plus extra for greasing
  • 2 whole large eggs, room temperature
  • 2 large egg yolks, room temperature
  • 80g (⅓ cup + 1 tbsp) caster or superfine sugar
  • Pinch of fine sea salt
  • 30g (¼ cup) all-purpose flour
  • 1 teaspoon pure vanilla extract
  • Unsweetened cocoa powder, for dusting ramekins
  • Flaky sea salt, to finish

Method

Prepare the ramekins
Preheat your oven to 425°F (220°C). Generously butter four 6-oz ramekins, making sure to coat the sides in upward strokes — this helps the cakes release cleanly. Dust each ramekin with cocoa powder, tapping out any excess. This step is non-negotiable: it's what allows the cakes to release without tearing.
Melt chocolate and butter
Combine chopped chocolate and butter in a heatproof bowl. Melt together either in the microwave in 30-second bursts (stirring between each) or over a double boiler with barely simmering water. Stir until completely smooth and glossy. Set aside to cool for 5 minutes — you don't want it to scramble the eggs.

Whisk eggs and sugar
In a large bowl, whisk together the whole eggs, egg yolks, sugar, salt, and vanilla extract vigorously for about 2 minutes until the mixture is pale, thick, and has more than doubled in volume. This aeration is what gives the cakes their delicate, slightly soufflé-like texture around the edges.
Combine and add flour
Pour the slightly cooled chocolate-butter mixture into the egg mixture and fold gently with a rubber spatula until just combined — don't overmix. Sift the flour over the top and fold again until no streaks of flour remain. The batter should be thick, glossy, and smooth.
Fill and bake
Divide the batter evenly among the prepared ramekins — they should be about ¾ full. Place on a baking sheet. At this point you can refrigerate them for up to 2 days; add 1–2 minutes to baking time if baking from cold. Bake at 425°F for exactly 11–12 minutes. The edges should be set and the tops matte, but the center should still have a slight wobble when you gently shake the pan.
Unmold and serve immediately
Remove from oven and let rest for exactly 1 minute. Run a thin knife around the edge of each ramekin. Place a serving plate upside-down over the ramekin and, in one confident motion, invert. Let it sit for 10 seconds, then lift the ramekin away. The cake should slide out cleanly. Finish with a pinch of flaky sea salt and serve immediately with vanilla ice cream or crème fraîche.

The Timing Secret: Every oven is different. The first time you make this recipe, bake a single test cake and cut it open — this tells you exactly how your oven behaves. 11 minutes gives you a fully molten center; 13 minutes gives you a fudgy-but-set center. Know your oven, then never waver from your tested time.
